The Florida Keys are the most reachable world class diving in the United States: a string of islands trailing off the southern tip of Florida, with warm water, shallow coral gardens, and a line of Spanish galleons and retired navy ships resting on the seabed. It is where many American divers first fall for the ocean, and it holds the only living coral barrier reef in the continental United States.

Reef condition

This reef has lost more than half its coral since 1987, after two big heat waves. Right now the water is warmer than usual, so expect some pale coral. But the shipwrecks and the giant goliath grouper are still here, because fishing is banned and the pressure is easing.

Coral cover over time

Live coral has fallen from 31% to 14% since 1987. If the decline holds, little would remain by around 2045.

Getting there ⌄
Fly into Miami (MIA) or Key West (EYW). From Miami it's roughly a 2 to 3 hour drive down the Overseas Highway to the dive towns; from Key West it's under an hour. From shore, operators reach the reef in 20 to 40 minutes by boat.
Where to stay ⌄
Most divers stay on land and dive with a local operator each morning, basing in Key Largo for the northern reefs or Big Pine Key near Looe Key. A few liveaboards run multi day trips out to the deeper wrecks like the Duane.
