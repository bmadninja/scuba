# Design Decision Log
**Product:** Scuba Season visual + UX rebrand
**Date:** 2026-06-19
**Spec version:** 1.0.0

This log records the key design decisions made during discovery and design handoff. Each entry states what was decided and why, so future contributors understand the reasoning rather than just the outcome.

---

## Visual direction

### Clean break from dark mode — not a toggle

**Decision:** The rebrand is light mode only. There is no dark mode, no dark-mode toggle, and no dark card variants. The only dark surface is the footer (`#14191E`).

**Reasoning:** The previous production design is dark with cyan accents. The rebrand direction is a full pivot to editorial, photo-led, light. Offering a toggle would fragment the design system, complicate photography contrast decisions, and imply the dark mode is maintained — it is not. Divers browsing on phones at the surface do not need a dark UI; they need fast, legible reef data.

---

### Yellow as the single brand accent

**Decision:** `#F6C700` (brand yellow) is the sole accent colour. It appears on CTAs, the logo mark, and 1.5px section divider rules. Nowhere else.

**Reasoning:** The temptation in a reef-themed product is to reach for ocean blue or teal as an accent. Those colours were reserved for the data-accent role (`#0E4F6E` for text links in prose). Yellow is warm, high-contrast against white, visually distinctive, and rare enough in marine-product design to be ownable. By constraining yellow to action elements only, every yellow element becomes a CTA signal — the user learns the system without being taught it.

---

### Dark footer is the only dark surface

**Decision:** `#14191E` for the footer background. Every other surface is `#FFFFFF`.

**Reasoning:** The footer needs to feel like a contained endpoint — a visual full stop at the bottom of every page. A dark footer achieves this without requiring a dark-mode system. No other sections get dark treatment, not even hero overlays (which use gradient overlays over photography, not coloured backgrounds).

---

## Navigation

### Upload CTA lives in the nav as a yellow button — not a text link

**Decision:** The upload action is a yellow button (`background: #F6C700`) in the top-right of the nav. It is not a fourth text link alongside Explore, Method, About.

**Reasoning:** Upload is not a browse destination — it is a call to action. Treating it as a nav text link would imply parity with informational pages (Explore, Method, About). The yellow button signals action and creates visual weight in the nav that draws the eye. On mobile it anchors the bottom of the hamburger drawer as a full-width button — still clearly a CTA, not a link.

---

### No yellow top announcement banner

**Decision:** No announcement banner at the top of any page. The nav is the top of the page.

**Reasoning:** A yellow announcement banner would violate the rule that yellow means action. A banner is not a CTA; it is information. Coloring it yellow would break the system. Additionally, banners compress viewport height, pushing hero photography down, which undermines the photo-first design pillar.

---

## Content strategy

### About page: intentionally text-heavy, founder narrative, not simplified

**Decision:** The About page is exempt from "less text, more visual flow." It carries Josie's personal voice, full narrative, and may be long by the standards of the rest of the site.

**Reasoning:** Divers, funders, researchers, and journalists who land on the About page are specifically seeking the human story behind the platform. Truncating or designing-away the narrative would undermine the nonprofit's authenticity positioning. The founder story is a differentiator, not a liability. The About page is the one place where emotional, personal, run-on prose is the product.

---

### Submission is a first-class peer to research — two equal CTAs on homepage

**Decision:** The homepage has two equal-weight CTAs: "Explore reefs" and "Upload a sighting." Neither is primary; neither is a ghost secondary. The ghost button is used for "Explore reefs" (white border on hero) and the yellow button for "Upload a sighting" — but this is a visual hierarchy driven by button style, not by intent. Both actions are positioned as the core product offering.

**Reasoning:** Scuba Season's value proposition has two equal pillars: (1) research tool for divers, (2) citizen science platform. Treating Upload as a secondary action would make the platform look like a data reader with an upload feature bolted on. Making both equal from the first screen communicates the full product model and supports the citizen science angle for funders and press.

---

### No IUCN codes in diver-facing copy

**Decision:** IUCN conservation status codes (NT, VU, EN, CR, etc.) are never displayed in diver-facing UI. If conservation status is shown, it is written in full plain English or omitted.

**Reasoning:** IUCN codes are meaningful to researchers and conservationists but opaque to recreational divers. Displaying them without explanation creates false authority and jargon clutter. They can be surfaced on the Method page where the audience expects technical rigour — not on species listings, site pages, or the explore grid.

---

### Common names only for species

**Decision:** Scientific names (binomial nomenclature) are not shown anywhere in the diver-facing UI. Species are identified by their common English name only.

**Reasoning:** The platform is for divers, not taxonomists. "Hawksbill sea turtle" communicates immediately. "Eretmochelys imbricata" does not. Scientific names can be present in the underlying data (for iNaturalist and GBIF broadcast) but are never surfaced to the user. The Method page is the exception — it may refer to scientific names in the context of explaining data sources.

## Typography

### Source Serif 4 + IBM Plex Sans + IBM Plex Mono type stack

**Decision:** Three typefaces, each with a distinct role. Source Serif 4 for display and headings. IBM Plex Sans for body and UI. IBM Plex Mono for data labels, eyebrows, stats, and badges.

**Reasoning:**
- Source Serif 4 (light italic) carries the editorial, National Geographic quality the rebrand is after. It is not a decorative serif — it is a workhorse display face with historical credibility.
- IBM Plex Sans is a humanist sans that reads cleanly at small sizes, which matters for metadata-heavy cards and filter pill labels.
- IBM Plex Mono gives data a distinct typographic register. When the user sees monospaced type, they know they are reading a data value — not a description. This is the key to the "data with dignity" pillar without requiring colour differentiation on every stat.

The three-face system is intentional and complete. No additional typefaces should be added.

## UX model

### No authentication required to browse

**Decision:** The entire site — all locations, all dive sites, all reef data, all species information — is accessible without sign-in. Authentication is required only to submit a sighting (Upload wizard, Step 3).

**Reasoning:** Scuba Season is a free public reef atlas. Gating research access behind a sign-in wall would contradict the nonprofit mission and reduce the value proposition for divers who just want to plan a trip. The sign-in gate at upload is justified because submitted sightings need to be attributed to a real account for iNaturalist, GBIF, and CoralWatch compliance.

---

### Skeleton loaders, not spinners

**Decision:** All loading states use skeleton cards (shimmer placeholders in the shape of the loaded content). Spinners are not used.

**Reasoning:** Spinners communicate waiting without context. Skeletons show the user what is about to appear and where, reducing perceived load time and preventing layout shift. Given that the Explore page loads a card grid and a map simultaneously, using skeletons for the card grid keeps the layout stable during load.

---

### Data unavailability: show last known + inline note, never hide

**Decision:** When a live data source is unavailable, the last-known value is shown in its normal position with a short inline note in IBM Plex Mono 11px muted type.

**Reasoning:** Hiding the element when data is unavailable creates a confusing layout hole and suggests the metric does not exist. Showing an error colour alarm is disproportionate — a data source being temporarily offline is a routine infrastructure event, not a crisis. The inline note approach is honest without being alarming, consistent with the "live data, no timestamps" principle.
